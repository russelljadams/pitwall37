# Ride Heights Basics
**Source:** Virtual Racing School (VRS)
**URL:** https://virtualracingschool.com/academy/iracing-career-guide/advanced-concepts/ride-heights/

## Overview

Ride height represents how far the chassis sits from the ground and is measured in millimeters or inches. It's one of the most impactful yet straightforward setup adjustments in sim racing. Converting between qualifying and race setups often requires only fuel adjustments and ride height modifications.

## Static vs. Dynamic Ride Heights

Static ride height is the garage setting you configure, while dynamic ride height refers to actual ground clearance as the vehicle navigates the track. Dynamic measurements fluctuate constantly — when the car passes over curbing, when aerodynamic forces compress springs, and as fuel consumption reduces vehicle weight throughout a race stint.

The relationship between sensor readings and actual ground clearance varies by car. For instance, splitter-mounted sensors may sit several centimeters above the splitter's lowest point, meaning a non-zero reading occurs even when bottoming out.

## Three Key Purposes

### 1. Lower Center of Gravity Effects

A lower sitting car generally has better handling because a lower center of gravity means less lateral weight transfer, reducing weight shifts during cornering and preserving available grip.

### 2. Aerodynamic Optimization

For downforce-dependent vehicles, ride height critically influences aerodynamic performance. Each car has an ideal range for maximum downforce and a separate range minimizing drag. These ranges may or may not overlap, requiring experimentation specific to each vehicle.

### 3. Ground Clearance Management

Preventing bottoming out on bumps and curbs protects handling integrity. When components lose track contact, tire loading decreases and speed diminishes significantly.

## Practical Example: Setup Conversion

Converting a qualifying setup to a race set typically means added fuel pushes the car below legal ride height specifications. Raising ride height requires decreasing perch offsets or increasing pushrod length on each wheel to pass technical inspection while matching the original qualifying ride heights.

For vehicles with off-center fuel tanks (like the BMW Z4 GT3), static ride heights with full tanks demand special consideration since fuel burn shifts weight distribution and increases ride height asymmetrically.

## Suspension Geometry Interactions

Adjustments aren't isolated. Changes to spring perch offset or pushrod length inevitably affect camber and toe alignment. Each modification requires verification and potential correction of related parameters through iterative adjustment.

## Related Adjustments to Monitor

- **Spring rates**: Stiffer springs elevate the car; softer springs lower it
- **Tire pressure**: Acts as a spring, influencing ride heights significantly
- **Camber and toe**: Modifications to these angles require concurrent ride height compensation
- **Fuel load**: Additional weight compresses springs, reducing ride heights

## Conclusion

Understanding ride height mechanics and their interactions with suspension geometry provides the foundation for effective setup work. Success requires extensive testing and experimentation with each specific vehicle to discover optimal configurations.
